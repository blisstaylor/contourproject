Kerrie Poliness

About the work

The artist will lead the production of large-scale ‘walking drawings’ on the paved ground surfaces at two sites. The public is invited to participate in making a large circular drawing in front of the National Library and long ‘wave’ drawings along University Avenue. No previous experience or artistic skills are required. The drawing process is open to all ages, wheelchair users are also welcome. The drawings are made using ‘jumbo’ chalk which is applied while walking and using specially designed drawing sticks designed and supplied by the artist. The pace of walking and drawing is self-directed. The lines of the drawing become pathways to follow which become more clearly defined as repeated layers of chalk are applied through drawing while walking. There is no limit to the number of people who can participate, however the scale of the drawing and physical distancing will limit the number of people who can be present on the drawing at one time, courteous behaviour and giving way to others is also required in order to keep the drawing in process.

About the artist

Kerrie Poliness combines everyday materials, geometric abstraction and DIY culture to develop paintings, sculptures, drawings, exhibitions, videos and instruction books which are used to make large-scale site responsive drawings onto wall and ground surfaces. In 1988 Poliness restricted her practice to focus on permutations of the diamond shape and associated intersecting diagonal lines. Poliness’s artworks explore interdependencies between people, objects, architectural space, nature and art. Appearing as wonky grid-like patterns, her instruction-based drawings visualise and demonstrate how asymmetry and diversity arise through the repeated application of systems to matter. They also demonstrate how to make and practice steering ‘adaptive grids’. Fast algorithms paired with meshes (grids) are increasingly used to compute and collect data to design and plan our world. Adaptive meshes help refine our interrelationships with the natural world. These drawing instruction books are slow algorithms which can be used to explore and visualise the nature of these processes.

Kerrie Poliness has exhibited extensively since 1987, her artworks are represented in public and private collections throughout Australia. She co-founded the artist initiatives Store 5 gallery (1989-91) with Gary Wilson and Melinda Harper and Circular magazine (1993 – 2000) with Marco Fusinato. Poliness has carried out various architectural, cultural, heritage and environmental projects in collaboration with a wide range of groups and individuals, particularly Melbourne’s Living Museum of the West (1994 - 2019) where she co-manages the arts programs. Poliness currently has a PhD under examination (2020), is a board member of West Space (2015-20) and is represented by Anna Schwartz Gallery.

Recent exhibitions include Never the Same River Anna Schwartz Gallery, Melbourne (2019); Today, Tomorrow, Yesterday, Museum of Contemporary Art, Sydney (2017-19); Play On: The art of sport (touring regional venues) (2018-19); Call of the Avant-Garde: Constructivism and Australian Art, Museum of Modern Art at Heide, Melbourne (2017); Every Brilliant Eye: Australian Art of the 1990s National Gallery of Victoria Australia, Melbourne (2017).
